COVID Orders Business Impact

Below is a structured compilation of key federal, state, county, and city COVID‐19 orders, proclamations, and public health directives from 2020–2021 that impacted businesses in Camarillo, California. Each entry includes the order name/number, enactment and rescission dates, any superseding orders, and a 3–5–sentence summary of the order’s provisions and its specific impact on businesses (with special emphasis on Q3 2020). In addition, an analysis is provided regarding possible reasons why these orders affected businesses during that quarter.

────────────────────────────────────────
Federal Orders

• Proclamation 9994 – “National Emergency Concerning COVID-19”
  Date Enacted: March 13, 2020
  Date Rescinded: Not rescinded during 2020–2021 (remained in effect; later continued by the President on Feb. 24, 2021)
  Rescinded By: Terminated later by Congress in 2023 (beyond the 2020–2021 scope)
  
  Summary: This proclamation declared a nationwide emergency due to the COVID-19 outbreak. It activated federal emergency powers and unlocked funding that, in turn, enabled state and local authorities to issue subsequent lockdowns and restrictions. Although the proclamation did not itself mandate business closures, it set the stage for the aggressive measures imposed by state and local governments. In Q3 2020, businesses in Camarillo operated under this ongoing national emergency—meaning that while federal support such as stimulus aid was available, the local operations continued to be limited by subordinate orders. The continued national emergency underscored both the severity of the pandemic and the legal basis for other measures.

(Link: https://bidenwhitehouse.archives.gov/briefing-room/presidential-actions/2021/02/24/notice-on-the-continuation-of-the-national-emergency-concerning-the-coronavirus-disease-2019-covid-19-pandemic/#:~:text=On%20March%2013%2C%202020%2C%20by,and%20safety%20of%20the%20Nation)

• CDC/FAA Mask Mandates – “Federal Mask Requirements for Travel”
  Date Enacted: January 21, 2021 (Executive Order 13998 on travel safety; effective February 1, 2021 via CDC Order)
  Date Rescinded: Not rescinded in 2021 (remained in effect throughout the year; later lifted in 2022)
  Rescinded By: A court order on April 18, 2022 ended the CDC travel mask mandate
  
  Summary: Although not directly aimed at private businesses, early 2021 brought federal mandates for mask use on airplanes, trains, buses, airports, and federal properties. These measures were introduced by President Biden through an executive order and implemented by the CDC to help secure safe travel. For Camarillo businesses in Q3 2020, however, these mandates had no direct ongoing effect because they were implemented later. They do, nonetheless, reflect the federal response’s evolution as the pandemic persisted into 2021.

California State Orders

• State of Emergency Proclamation – “California COVID-19 State of Emergency”
  Order Number: N/A (Governor’s Proclamation)
  Date Enacted: March 4, 2020
  Date Rescinded: Not rescinded during 2020–2021 (state of emergency remained active throughout that period)
  Rescinded By: Ended February 28, 2023 by a subsequent proclamation
                        
  Summary: Governor Gavin Newsom declared a state of emergency on March 4, 2020 in response to the rapidly spreading COVID-19 outbreak. This proclamation allowed the state to mobilize additional resources, suspend some statutory requirements, and paved the way for later public health orders affecting business operations. Throughout Q3 2020, the emergency status provided the legal backing for continued health directives that profoundly affected business activities. Its ongoing presence helped justify strict actions aimed at protecting public health even as economic pressures mounted.

(Link: https://www.gov.ca.gov/2020/03/04/governor-newsom-declares-state-of-emergency-to-help-state-prepare-for-broader-spread-of-covid-19/#:~:text=SACRAMENTO%20%E2%80%93%20As%20part%20of)

• Executive Order N-33-20 – “Statewide ‘Stay-at-Home’ Order”
  Date Enacted: March 19, 2020 (effective immediately)
  Date Rescinded: June 15, 2021
  Rescinded By: Executive Order N-07-21 (June 11, 2021) which terminated the order effective June 15, 2021; also superseded by phased reopening orders
  
  Summary: This order was California’s sweeping lockdown order at the onset of the pandemic, instructing nearly every resident to remain at home except for essential needs. It forced non-essential businesses (such as dine-in restaurants, retail stores, gyms, salons, etc.) to either close or drastically reduce operations while allowing only essential services (food stores, pharmacies, banks, etc.) to remain open. By Q3 2020 the direct effects had evolved (with some phased reopenings granted), yet many business sectors continued limited operations because their reopening was based on the remnants or modifications of this original order. The order’s persistence until mid-2021 highlights why businesses had to cope with severe restrictions during Q3 2020.

(Link: https://www.gov.ca.gov/wp-content/uploads/2020/03/3.19.20-attested-EO-N-33-20-COVID-19-HEALTH-ORDER.pdf#:~:text=March%2019%2C%202020)

• State Public Health Officer Order (Stage 2 Reopening) – “Modifying Stay-at-Home (Early Stage 2)”
  Order Date/Number: May 7, 2020 (issued under Executive Order N-60-20)
  Date Rescinded: June 15, 2021 (formally repealed by the Beyond Blueprint Order)
  Rescinded By: “Beyond Blueprint” State Public Health Officer Order (June 11, 2021)
  
  Summary: As part of California’s gradual reopening plan, this May 7 order laid the groundwork for Stage 2 by easing some restrictions from the March stay-at-home order. It allowed lower-risk businesses (such as many retail stores) to reopen under modified conditions like curbside pickup, while higher-risk venues (dine-in restaurants, gyms, salons) remained closed. For Camarillo businesses, this represented the beginning of a cautious reopening in Q3 2020—many had opened at reduced capacity in June only to face subsequent rollback when COVID-19 cases surged again in July. The order thus both provided an initial economic lifeline and contributed to the later volatility experienced in the summer.

• State Public Health Officer Order (July 13, 2020) – “Statewide Rollback of Reopening”
  Order Date: July 13, 2020 
  Date Rescinded: Superseded on August 31, 2020 by the Blueprint tier framework (and formally rescinded June 15, 2021)
  Rescinded By: The State Public Health Officer “Blueprint” Order (August 28, 2020) and later by the Beyond Blueprint Order
  
  Summary: Following a sharp July rise in COVID-19 cases, this order re-imposed closures and restrictions on businesses that had reopened during Stage 2. It mandated the closing of indoor operations across many sectors (e.g., shutting down indoor dining, bars, fitness centers, and personal care services) even if they had briefly reopened. In Camarillo, this resulted in many restaurants and other indoor business venues having to revert to takeout or completely stop operations—even after reopening a few weeks earlier. The sudden rollback was a direct response to the surge in infections and was a key factor in making Q3 2020 one of significant economic and operational challenge for local businesses.

• “Blueprint for a Safer Economy” Framework – “Color-Tier Reopening System”
  Order Number: State Public Health Officer Order (August 28, 2020)
  Date Enacted: Announced August 28, 2020 and effective August 31, 2020
  Date Rescinded: June 15, 2021
  Rescinded By: The Beyond Blueprint Order (June 15, 2021) ended the tier system
  
  Summary: The Blueprint for a Safer Economy replaced previous blanket closure orders with a risk‐based, color‑coded tier system. Each county was assigned a tier (Purple for widespread risk, Red for substantial, Orange for moderate, and Yellow for minimal risk) which determined exactly which businesses could operate and under what conditions. Ventura County (encompassing Camarillo) initially entered the most restrictive Purple tier on August 31, 2020. Although this framework allowed for some indoor operations under stricter capacity limits and safety measures, it meant that in Q3 2020 most Camarillo businesses operated under very tight constraints. The tier system provided predictability but also meant that many sectors remained shuttered or restricted until local data improved.

• Regional Stay-at-Home Order – “ICU Capacity Triggered Shutdown”
  Order Date: December 3, 2020 (announced; triggered in the Southern California region on Dec 6, 2020)
  Date Rescinded: January 25, 2021
  Rescinded By: Lifting of the Regional Stay-at-Home Order by state public health officials on January 25, 2021 once ICU projections improved
  
  Summary: In response to a severe winter surge in COVID-19, California imposed a Regional Stay-at-Home Order that was triggered when ICU bed capacity in the region fell below 15%. In the Southern California region (which includes Ventura County), this order forced the closure of nearly all indoor business operations, including even outdoor operations for certain sectors like restaurants (restricted to takeout only), personal care services, and more. Although this harsh measure was implemented in December 2020 (Q4), its impending threat and the environment it created were influenced by the summer’s struggles. Businesses in Q3 2020 were already facing widespread uncertainty, and the looming possibility of another full shutdown was part of the overall economic challenge.

• “Beyond the Blueprint” Reopening Order – (Executive Order N-07-21 / State Health Officer Order)
  Date Enacted: June 11, 2021 (signed; effective June 15, 2021)
  Date Rescinded: N/A (this order itself terminated all prior COVID-related business restrictions)
  Rescinded By: This order rescinded the March stay-at-home order and the Blueprint tier framework
  
  Summary: This order marked California’s full reopening phase on June 15, 2021 by formally rescinding the earlier orders and setting businesses free to operate at near-normal capacity. While not part of Q3 2020, it provides context: the lingering restrictions of Q3 2020 remained in place until conditions allowed such a comprehensive reopening nearly three quarters later. The necessity of maintaining restrictions in Q3 2020, even though they eventually ended in June 2021, explains why business conditions were so challenging during that quarter.

Ventura County Orders

• Ventura County Local Health Emergency – “Declaration of Local Health Emergency”
  Order/Proclamation Date: March 12, 2020
  Date Rescinded: Not rescinded in 2020–2021 (remained in effect; finally ended in 2022, outside our timeframe)
  Rescinded By: A later Ventura County Board of Supervisors resolution (March 2022)
  
  Summary: On March 12, 2020, Ventura County’s Public Health Officer declared a local health emergency. While this declaration did not itself impose closures, it provided the legal authority for the county to issue subsequent directives and enforce state orders. For businesses in Camarillo, the local emergency meant that the county was empowered to act swiftly and impose additional local rules as needed. The persistence of this emergency throughout 2020 justified the continued stringent measures and special enforcement actions that affected business operations in Q3.

• Ventura County Public Health Order (March 17, 2020) – “Initial County Closures/Gathering Restrictions”
  Order Date: March 17, 2020
  Date Rescinded: Superseded by the “Stay Well at Home” order on March 20, 2020 (and later replaced by additional orders)
  Rescinded By: Ventura County Health Officer Order of March 20, 2020
  
  Summary: Before the statewide stay-at-home order took effect, Ventura County issued its own order on March 17, 2020 that urged vulnerable populations to shelter in place and directed the closure of certain businesses and public facilities. The order targeted high-risk venues such as bars, gyms, and movie theaters. While it was quickly incorporated into the broader county order on March 20, it set an early tone for the aggressive local response that continued to shape business operations throughout Q3 2020.

• “Stay Well at Home” Order (Ventura County) – “Countywide Stay-at-Home Order”
  Order Date: March 20, 2020
  Date Rescinded: June 11, 2021 (implicitly, when aligning with state reopening; formally lifted by the June 15, 2021 update)
  Rescinded By: Ventura County Health Officer Order (June 15, 2021)
  
  Summary: This order required all Ventura County residents to remain in their homes except for essential activities and prompted the closure of non-essential businesses. In Camarillo, almost all in-person business operations were halted unless they were part of the essential services. Even though modifications were later introduced (allowing limited operations), the foundational restrictions of this order meant that for much of Q3 2020, businesses operated under the shadow of the original lockdown’s limitations. Its extended duration was due to persistently high COVID-19 case counts in the county.

• Ventura County Public Health Order Updates (March–April 2020) – “Extensions and Social Distancing Protocols”
  Order Dates:
    – March 31, 2020: Extension of the Stay Well at Home order until April 19, 2020
    – April 9, 2020: Update adding a ban on gatherings of any size outside a single household and mandating social distancing protocols for essential businesses
    – April 20, 2020: Further extension of the stay-at-home order through May 15, 2020
  Date Rescinded: Superseded by later reopening orders (May 2020 and beyond; fully repealed on May 7, 2020 when replaced by state-aligned “Safely Reopening” order)
  Rescinded By: Ventura County Health Officer Order on May 7, 2020
  
  Summary: During the spring of 2020, Ventura County repeatedly updated its stay-at-home directive to extend its duration and to tighten safety measures—including bans on gatherings and requirements for social distancing at essential businesses. Although these specific orders were replaced on May 7, 2020, their legacy endured. The protocols set a baseline that continued to influence operational rules for businesses as they reopened in Q3 2020. In many cases, businesses had to keep following strict social distancing and operational guidelines first established by these spring orders, even after official restrictions had eased.

• “Safely Reopening Ventura County” Order – “Stage 2 County Reopening (May 2020)”
  Order Dates: Initially May 7, 2020 – and an amendment on May 20, 2020
  Date Rescinded: Superseded by the state Blueprint framework (effective August 31, 2020) and finally ended with the June 15, 2021 full reopening
  Rescinded By: Ventura County Health Officer Order dated August 31, 2020 (aligning the county with the state’s Blueprint Tier 1) and then the June 15, 2021 order
  
  Summary: In early May 2020, Ventura County initiated a pathway for reopening with the “Safely Reopening” orders. The initial May 7 order replaced stricter directives from April with conditions that allowed lower-risk businesses to reopen with COVID-19 safety protocols. An amendment on May 20, 2020 permitted additional sectors such as dine-in restaurants and in-store retail to resume operations under controlled circumstances. For Q3 2020 Camarillo businesses, these orders meant that by summer, many could reopen—albeit with reduced capacity and additional safety measures. However, the reopening was precarious; when the pandemic surged again in July, many restrictions were reimposed, making Q3 a roller-coaster period for local commerce.

• Ventura County 4th of July Closure Order – “Temporary Beach Closure (Holiday Weekend)”
  Order Date: June 30, 2020 (for the July 3–5, 2020 weekend)
  Date Rescinded: Automatically expired on July 6, 2020
  Rescinded By: The order’s expiration at its designated end date
  
  Summary: Anticipating large crowds and the associated risk of virus spread over the Independence Day weekend, the Ventura County Health Officer ordered a temporary closure of beaches and restrictions on beach parking. While primarily aimed at preventing mass gatherings on the coast, the order indirectly affected local and regional tourism-related businesses. For residents of Camarillo and nearby areas, such targeted closures meant a short-term reduction in holiday-related spending and activities. This measure is emblematic of the targeted, sometimes unpredictable, disruptions that compounded the economic challenges businesses endured during Q3 2020.

• Ventura County July 2020 Order – “Local Implementation of State Rollback”
  Order Date: July 13, 2020 (effective July 14, 2020)
  Date Rescinded: Partially rescinded on October 6, 2020 as the county transitioned to a less restrictive tier (Red tier)
  Rescinded By: Ventura County Health Officer Order on October 6, 2020
  
  Summary: This local order was Ventura County’s means of enforcing the governor’s July 13 statewide rollback. It mandated that various sectors such as gyms, personal care services, places of worship, and similar businesses shut down indoor operations immediately—even if they had only recently reopened. In practical terms, many Camarillo businesses (fitness centers, nail salons, tattoo parlors, etc.) that had begun to gain footing in early summer were forced to close or shift to outdoor-only modes. The swift imposition of such restrictive measures in July 2020 made Q3 extremely challenging for many in the business community, as operations had to be repeatedly reconfigured amidst an uncertain public health environment.

• Ventura County “Red Tier” Reopening Order – “Easing Restrictions in Fall 2020”
  Order Date: October 6, 2020
  Date Rescinded: Remained effective until further state orders (later formally superseded by the December Regional Stay-at-Home Order and later by statewide changes)
  Rescinded By: Effective changes later in December 2020 and ultimately by the June 15, 2021 state reopening
  
  Summary: By early October 2020, the COVID-19 situation in Ventura County had improved enough to allow a transition from the most restrictive (Purple) tier to the less restrictive (Red) tier. The Health Officer’s October 6 order rescinded parts of the July closures and permitted certain businesses—gyms, houses of worship, and personal care services—to reopen for limited indoor operations under strict protocol. For local businesses, this order represented the first easing after months of severe restrictions, and many began noticing a modest recovery in customer traffic in the latter part of Q3 2020. However, the overall environment remained fragile, and further adjustments were imposed in December as conditions deteriorated once again.

• Ventura County Eviction Moratorium – “Temporary Ban on Evictions (Rent Deferral)”
  Order/Ordinance Number: Urgency Ordinance No. 4563 (with subsequent extensions)
  Date Enacted: April 22, 2020 (retroactive to March 2020; initially passed March 31, 2020)
  Date Rescinded: Residential protections replaced on September 30, 2020 (for commercial evictions, protections extended until that date; later state law took over for most cases)
  Rescinded By: California COVID-19 Tenant Relief Act (AB 3088) for residential tenants on Aug. 31, 2020; for commercial, Ventura County allowed the extension to expire as noted
  
  Summary: Recognizing the severe financial impact of the pandemic, Ventura County introduced an eviction moratorium to protect both residential tenants and small-business commercial tenants from being evicted due to nonpayment caused by COVID-related income losses. In Camarillo, this measure provided a necessary temporary relief by allowing businesses to delay rent payments, thus avoiding permanent closure due to eviction during the rough mid-pandemic period. Although this was not a rent forgiveness measure, the temporary deferral gave many businesses crucial time to adjust, restructure, or await a more favorable reopening environment in Q3 2020.

City of Camarillo Orders

• Local Emergency Proclamation (Camarillo) – “City Declaration of Emergency”
  Order Number: EOC Director Order No. 20-1 (ratified by City Council Resolution 20-36)
  Date Enacted: March 13, 2020 
  Date Rescinded: Not rescinded in 2020–2021 (local emergency remained active into 2021; formally ended in 2022)
  Rescinded By: A subsequent Camarillo City Council resolution in 2022
  
  Summary: On March 13, 2020, the City of Camarillo declared a local state of emergency in response to COVID-19. This measure activated the city’s Emergency Operations Center and allowed for the rapid deployment of resources and the adoption of additional local measures to protect public health. The emergency status also meant that city regulatory relief measures could be enacted for local businesses—for example, easing permit requirements. For business owners in Q3 2020, the continuing declaration indicated that the city was still in crisis mode, which influenced both public behavior and regulatory actions.

• Camarillo Eviction Moratorium Orders – “Citywide Moratorium on Evictions”
  Order Numbers: EOC Director Order No. 20-2 initially on March 18, 2020, amended on April 23, 2020
  Date Enacted: March 18, 2020 (amended April 23, 2020)
  Date Rescinded: Superseded by state law for residential tenants on Sept. 2020 and eventually ended with the local emergency
  Rescinded By: California AB 3088 (residential) and, for any remaining commercial provisions, by the ultimate termination of the local emergency
  
  Summary: The City of Camarillo enacted its own eviction moratorium to safeguard local tenants (both residential and commercial) from being evicted due to pandemic-related income losses. By delaying the eviction process, the city aimed to prevent a cascading economic crisis that could force permanent business closures as well as residential displacement. For Camarillo businesses operating in Q3 2020, this provided some temporary security against the compounding financial pressures of lost revenue amid restricting orders. Business owners could use this period to adjust their operations without facing the immediate threat of eviction.

• Camarillo Resolution No. 2020-63 – “Temporary Outdoor Dining Program”
  Date Enacted: Approximately June 10, 2020
  Date Rescinded: Expired with the end of COVID-19 capacity restrictions (effectively June 15, 2021; formally tied to the duration of the local emergency)
  Rescinded By: Automatically sunset when the city’s COVID emergency and related temporary zoning measures ended
  
  Summary: In June 2020, the Camarillo City Council passed Resolution 2020-63 to waive certain zoning requirements and fees for restaurants, permitting them to set up temporary outdoor dining areas in parking lots, sidewalks, or other open spaces. This emergency measure was designed to help restaurants continue serving customers when indoor dining was still prohibited or severely restricted. For many Camarillo eateries during Q3 2020, these outdoor dining spaces proved essential to generating any revenue, helping them to mitigate the effects of prolonged indoor closures. The resolution is an example of how the city sought to balance strict public health requirements with economic survival for local businesses.

• Miscellaneous City Measures – “Additional Camarillo Actions”
  Dates: Various during spring–summer 2020
  Summary: Apart from the emergency declarations and eviction moratoriums, Camarillo also implemented several supportive measures. These included suspending late fees for business taxes, moving permit processes online to reduce in-person interactions, and promoting take-out dining campaigns to sustain local restaurants. Such actions, while not formal “orders” in the same sense as closures, were part of the broader regulatory framework affecting local businesses during Q3 2020. Together, these measures illustrate how city-level actions complemented those at the county and state levels to both restrict and support economic activity during the pandemic.

Additional Notes on Q3 2020 Impacts

Taken together, these federal, state, county, and city measures created a highly complex regulatory environment in which Camarillo businesses had to operate during the third quarter of 2020. Even as some sectors began reopening under the “Safely Reopening” orders and the Blueprint framework, the rekindled wave in July and late summer led to rapid rollbacks, particularly for indoor operations. Meanwhile, supportive measures like eviction moratoriums and temporary outdoor dining resolutions provided both a cushion and an incentive for businesses to adapt quickly. The overall effect was that, throughout Q3 2020, many businesses had to adjust continuously—balancing strict public health requirements with the pressing need to generate revenue—all while facing a constantly changing legal framework.
